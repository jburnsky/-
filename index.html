Research

My primary field is sentence processing. I take insights from Linguistic Theory and Cognitive Psychology to study the processes that enable us to understand sentences. My research can be broken down into the following interconnected streams:

Predictability in Humans and Language Models

A word's predictability in its context has dramatic effects on its processing. For instance, predictable words are read faster than unpredictable ones.

  1. I made a trip to Costco to buy an excessive amount of maple syrup.
  2. I tend to cover my pancakes with an excessive amount of maple syrup.

"maple syrup" is read faster when in context #2 because it has been predicted (or preactivated) to a higher degree.

I study two aspects of this: what is it that is being predicted and what information is used to generate those predictions? I've used EEG, eye tracking while reading and a battery of behavioral experiments to answer these questions.

I am also interested in comparing predictions that people make to predictions that computers (Language Models) make. I have directly compared human sentence continuations to continuations provided by BERT (Devlin et al. 2018) and also to a novel bag-of-words language model I developed to get insights into what kinds of information seem to be prioritized and represented in humans and computers.

Thematic Role Processing

Sentences are rich with information for us to extract and interpret. One core piece of information we must interpret is how the noun(s) in the sentence relate to the verb(s); e.g. who is doing what to whom. While these thematic relations are often ultimately unambiguous, they can be challenging to interpret incrementally, especially when half way through a non-SVO sentence such as an embedded wh-question (as in below).

  1. I saw which seal the fish was chasing. (This isn't typically how the world works)
  2. I saw which fish the seal was chasing. (This is more typical: the seal is the 'chaser' and the fish is the 'chasee')

My research investigates what intermediate representations arise in sentences of this type and how we ultimately understand them.

The Implementation of the Binding Principles

Syntacticians and Semanticists have formulated various rules and constraints that ensure that when we get a sentence with a pronoun or a reflexive, we are guided to interpreting it in particular ways. For example, we are dissuaded from interpreting "her" as referring back to "Callie" in sentence 1, but not in sentence 2:

  1. Callie loves her. (her ≠ Callie)
  2. Callie wonders if Nora loves her. (her = Callie)

"Principle B," which postulates which entities a pronoun such as "her" in the example above can refer back to, is one such descriptive rule linguists have come up with. In work with Dr. Brian Dillon, I am investigating how exactly this rule is implemented algorithmically by using the visual world paradigm.
